## Which Direct Deposit Sources Actually Count (and Which Get Rejected)

A qualifying direct deposit is an ACH credit that the bank's system codes as payroll or an equivalent electronic transfer. The key word is "codes." Banks do not check whether money came from an employer. They check the ACH transaction code and originator metadata. This is why some brokerage ACH transfers trigger bonuses and others do not.

Using the wrong source means the deposit arrives in your account but never counts toward the requirement stack. The clock keeps ticking. The bonus window closes. The capital stays locked with nothing to show for it.

### SoFi

Employer payroll is the safest path. Wealthfront ACH transfers have 2 confirmed reports of triggering the bonus (0.90 confidence). Chase ACH transfers also have 2 confirmed reports (0.92 confidence). Cash App has 1 confirmed report (0.86 confidence). PayPal has 1 confirmed report (0.84 confidence). Fidelity and Schwab results are mixed -- some succeed, some do not. Venmo has 1 report of working (0.82 confidence) but thin direct deposit evidence makes it risky. P2P transfers generally fail across the board.

### Capital One 360

Fidelity ACH has 2 confirmed reports. Schwab ACH has 2 confirmed reports. Ally ACH has 2 confirmed reports. Employer payroll works. Internal Capital One transfers are explicitly excluded in the terms -- transferring from a Capital One savings account to the 360 Checking does not count. This catches people who already bank with Capital One. Venmo and Chase have mixed reports with low confidence.

### BMO

Employer payroll is the only reliably safe source. BMO's terms and conditions explicitly ban brokerage ACH transfers. Fidelity and Schwab have mixed community direct deposit evidence (confidence 0.72 and 0.71 respectively) -- some reports show success, but failures are also documented. PayPal fails (0 confirmed reports). Wells Fargo fails (0 confirmed reports). This is the strictest DD policy of the four.

### Chime

Gig economy ACH from Uber and DoorDash payouts qualifies. Cash App works (1 confirmed report). Venmo does not count. Chime accepts the widest range of ACH sources outside of traditional payroll.

## ChexSystems and EWS Screening: Who Gets Approved and Who Gets Denied

ChexSystems and Early Warning Services (EWS) are consumer reporting agencies that track banking history. Unpaid negative balances, involuntary account closures, and fraud flags all show up on these reports. Banks pull one or both during the application process. Chex/EWS sensitivity varies widely across these four bonuses.

The four bonuses on this list fall into three screening tiers.

### Tier 1: No Screening (SoFi, Chime)

SoFi runs a soft pull only. No ChexSystems query. No EWS query. Applicants with negative banking history, prior involuntary closures, or ChexSystems records can still get approved. This makes SoFi the lowest-friction option for anyone who has been denied a bank account elsewhere.

Chime runs no ChexSystems and no EWS screening. It is the most accessible bank account on this list. Chime positions itself as an alternative for the underbanked, and the screening policy reflects that. Zero Chex/EWS sensitivity on either account.

### Tier 2: EWS-Sensitive (Capital One 360)

Capital One pulls EWS during the application process. Confirmed EWS pulls and denials have been reported by applicants. If you have negative items on your EWS report, Capital One 360 may deny the application. Per community reports, ChexSystems is not the primary denial factor, so ChexSystems-only flags may not affect approval.

### Tier 3: ChexSystems (BMO)

BMO pulls ChexSystems. This is the most aggressive screening on the list and produces the highest denial rate. Applicants with any ChexSystems record -- even resolved items -- may face denial. Apply to BMO only if you have a clean banking history across both ChexSystems and EWS.

The practical order: if Chex/EWS sensitivity is a concern, start with Chime or SoFi. If approved, consider Capital One 360. BMO should be last.

## Effective Bonus APR: Why a $350 Bonus Can Beat a $400 Bonus

Effective bonus APR answers one question: how hard is your capital working during the lockup period? The formula:

**Effective Bonus APR = (Bonus Amount / Capital Lockup) x (365 / Days Locked) x 100**

This is not a savings account APY. It is not compounding. It measures the one-time payout relative to capital as if that payout were annualized, making different bonuses directly comparable.

### Worked Examples

**Chime:** $250 bonus / $500 capital lockup x (365 / 30 days) = 608.0% effective bonus APR. The low capital requirement and short window create an outsized annualized figure. The additional $100 in purchase cashback pushes the ceiling higher but depends on spend.

**SoFi:** $400 bonus / $5,000 capital lockup x (365 / 25 days) = 116.8% effective bonus APR. The 25-day window is the fastest payout. Capital is free again before most people's next credit card statement closes. Net bonus after fees: $400 (zero monthly fee drag).

**Capital One 360:** $250 bonus / $1,000 total DD requirement x (365 / 75 days) = 121.7% on the deposit amount. But the 6-month holding period is the real constraint. Factoring the full 180-day hold: $250 / $1,000 x (365 / 180) = 50.7%. The effective bonus APR of 100.0% in the table reflects the blended requirement stack.

**BMO:** $400 bonus / $4,000 capital lockup x (365 / 90 days) = 40.6% effective bonus APR. Highest dollar bonus tied with SoFi, but the 90-day window and $4,000 capital lockup produce the lowest effective bonus APR per dollar deployed.

## 5 Mistakes That Kill Your Online Bank Bonus (and How to Avoid Them)

### Mistake 1: Using Venmo or P2P as a Qualifying Direct Deposit

Venmo fails at nearly all four banks on this list (one thin report exists for SoFi, but confidence is low). P2P transfers from Zelle, PayPal (person-to-person), and similar apps also fail at SoFi and BMO. Direct deposit evidence from the community is clear on this point. Switch to employer payroll or a confirmed brokerage ACH source before starting the clock.

### Mistake 2: Applying to BMO with a ChexSystems Record

BMO pulls ChexSystems. Denials are reported even for resolved items. If you have any ChexSystems history, applying to BMO wastes time and creates an unnecessary inquiry on your report. Check your ChexSystems report first (free annual report at chexsystems.com) before applying.

### Mistake 3: Missing the DD Window

SoFi gives 25 days. Chime gives 30 days. Capital One gives 75 days. BMO gives 90 days. These windows start when the account opens, not when you decide to fund it. Set up your qualifying direct deposit before or immediately after opening the account. A payroll cycle that lands on day 26 at SoFi means $0 in bonus amount.

### Mistake 4: Closing Capital One Before the Safe-Close Date

Capital One 360 enforces a 6-month holding period. Closing the account before that safe-close date triggers a bonus clawback -- Capital One will reverse the $250. Mark your calendar for the safe-close date: 6 months after the bonus posts, not 6 months after account opening.

### Mistake 5: Forgetting Tax Implications

Bank bonuses are taxable income. The 2026 1099-MISC threshold rose to $2,000, so bonuses under $2,000 may not generate a form. The income is still reportable. Budget 22-37% for federal plus state taxes. On $1,400 in gross bonuses, expect to owe $308-$518 depending on your bracket and state.
